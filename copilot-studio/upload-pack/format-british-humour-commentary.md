# British Humour Commentary

## Purpose

Write a short public-interest commentary that blends dry British wit with a
serious practical argument.

This format is opinionated by design. If it reads like a generic witty
commentary, it has failed.

## Default Pairing

- default speaker: Sir Alistair Pembroke
- default media context: British Humour
- suitable channels: short video, podcast segment, social-ready commentary
- default intensity: Strong

## Required Intake

- source issue or trigger
- main practical argument
- preferred length or channel if relevant
- humour intensity if the user explicitly requests `Light` or `Strong`
- any sensitive topics that require care

## Output Format

```text
BRITISH HUMOUR COMMENTARY
[Speaker] | [Channel or use case]
***

HOOK
[One sharp opening line or contradiction]

OBSERVED ABSURDITY
[One or two lines that politely expose the contradiction, delay, or official absurdity]

COMMENTARY
[2 to 4 short paragraphs that turn the issue into a vivid, witty, serious point]

DEADPAN TURN
[One line that escalates the absurdity with calm seriousness and makes the
audience feel the system has become faintly ridiculous]

CLOSING LINE
[One memorable final line that lands on a practical conclusion]

OPTIONAL CUTDOWN
[One short quote or caption line for clips or social use]
```

## Rules

- Keep the voice dry, intelligent, and lightly ironic.
- Make the tone feel unmistakably British, sardonic, and raised-eyebrow rather
  than neutral or diplomatic.
- Use humour to sharpen the point, not replace it.
- Make the underlying argument clearly relevant to wind energy, BWEC's sector
  mission, or the delivery conditions for the wind sector.
- If the source material is broad, turn the joke-bearing contradiction into a
  practical point about wind energy, delivery, infrastructure, or industrial
  common sense.
- Include at least one elegant line that punctures bureaucratic delay,
  confusion, or institutional theatre.
- Include one deadpan turn or absurd comparison that lifts the piece above a
  generic opinion-column tone.
- Prefer one strong argument over several weaker ones.
- Make the ending clear, quotable, and serious in substance.
- Default to Sir Alistair Pembroke unless the user explicitly asks for another voice.
- Treat the format selection itself as sufficient instruction to activate the
  full Pembroke register.
- Do not drift into parody, slapstick, or mockery for its own sake.
- If a draft reads like polished institutional commentary, sharpen the irony
  once before finalizing it.
- If it still sounds merely respectable, increase the absurdity of the contrast
  rather than adding more jokes.

## Intensity Modes

### British Humour Light

Use this when the user explicitly asks for `Light`.

- keep the satire restrained, polished, and essayistic
- use dry irony without a strong absurdist turn
- make the piece feel clever and perceptive rather than overtly comic
- the `DEADPAN TURN` can stay subtle

### British Humour Strong

Use this when the user explicitly asks for `Strong` or when no intensity is
specified.

- use the full Pembroke register
- include a clear deadpan turn or absurd comparison
- let the institutional logic feel more obviously ridiculous
- make the result sharper, stranger, and more memorable than the light mode
