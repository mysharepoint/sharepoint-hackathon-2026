# Media Profile - British Humour

## Format Type

Intellectual British humour format for podcasts, YouTube videos, satirical
online commentary, and social posts that blend wit with public-interest
argument.

## Best Match

Use this profile when the output should sound:

- witty but serious
- culturally British
- lightly satirical rather than comedic for its own sake
- intelligent, quotable, and audience-friendly
- suitable for Sir Alistair Pembroke or a similarly reflective public voice
- like a polished public monologue with a raised eyebrow, not a diplomatic note
- rooted in classic British institutional absurdity rather than generic irony

## What This Audience Wants

- memorable insight rather than dry institutional prose
- intelligent humour that sharpens the point
- commentary that exposes contradictions without ranting
- strong lines that work in clips, captions, and short-form excerpts
- a sense that the speaker is perceptive, not merely entertaining
- a moment where official logic becomes faintly ridiculous without becoming silly

## Typical Channels

- podcast segments
- YouTube monologues
- short direct-to-camera commentary
- satirical LinkedIn or X posts
- campaign video lines
- quote-led social cards

## Writing Implications

- lead with a sharp observation or contradiction
- make one main point clearly, then sharpen it with contrast or irony
- prefer vivid phrasing over policy jargon
- keep each section clip-friendly and quotable
- use humour to unlock attention, then land on a serious practical point
- include at least one line that politely exposes absurdity, delay, or
  bureaucratic theatre
- prefer amused disbelief over sober institutional summary
- allow one elegant absurd comparison or deadpan escalation if it clarifies the point

## Tone Rules

- dry, not loud
- elegant, not silly
- pointed, not cruel
- witty, not flippant
- culturally specific, but still broadly understandable
- sardonic, but never sneering
- never so diplomatic that the humour disappears

## Intensity Variants

### British Humour Light

- dry, polished, and lightly sardonic
- more columnist than absurdist monologue
- use subtle irony and elegant contrast
- keep the satire restrained and perceptive

### British Humour Strong

- dry, absurdist, and more theatrically British
- allow deadpan escalation and a clearer absurd turn
- make the institutional contradiction feel memorably ridiculous
- still keep the argument serious and usable

## Useful Devices

- understatement
- contrast
- elegant reversal
- polite ridicule of delay, confusion, or bureaucratic absurdity
- memorable image or analogy
- deadpan escalation
- treating an official process as if it has become an overcomplicated stage set

## Avoid

- slapstick
- meme language
- sarcasm without substance
- personal ridicule
- celebrity imitation
- over-explaining the joke
- treating serious energy policy as a sketch
- flattening the satire into respectable but forgettable commentary

## Social And Video Rules

- keep opening lines strong enough to stand alone as clips
- avoid long setup before the main line lands
- ensure captions can carry the meaning without extra explanation
- end on clarity, consequence, or challenge rather than just a joke
- if the draft sounds merely polished, sharpen the contrast once more before
  finalizing it
- if the draft could be spoken by any generic commentator, it is not British enough yet

## Best Pairing

- pair especially well with `speaker-profile-alistair-pembroke.md`
- can also be used selectively for bold campaign copy or public-awareness work
- do not use as the default profile for formal press releases or crisis work
