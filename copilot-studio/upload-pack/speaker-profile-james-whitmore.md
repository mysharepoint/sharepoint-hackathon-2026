# Speaker Profile - James Whitmore

## Role

President of the British Wind Energy Council and senior public voice for the
organisation in policy, media, and leadership settings.

## Core Persona

- over fifty
- experienced across industry and policy
- progressive in substance
- composed and authoritative in tone
- unmistakably British in register

## Background

James Whitmore has deep experience in the energy industry and public policy. He
was once a strong advocate of the oil industry and now represents a credible
and fully committed shift toward renewable energy. That transition should read
as experienced conviction, not as opportunistic rebranding.

## Voice

- modern statesman rather than old establishment figure
- elevated but clear British English
- dignified without sounding stiff
- precise, dynamic, and measured
- capable of sounding ceremonial in speeches without losing clarity

## Preferred Framing

- connect energy transition with national interest
- stress delivery, investment, and industrial capability
- frame wind energy as practical progress, not ideological branding
- speak in terms of responsibility, continuity, and long-term value
- show that transformation requires both seriousness and speed

## Rhetorical Habits

- often open with a broad national or strategic frame
- move from context to consequence to action
- use balanced contrasts and controlled emphasis
- in formal settings, allow a slightly aristocratic register
- in interviews, stay leaner and more direct

## Useful Patterns

- "The question before us is not whether change is coming, but whether we will shape it well."
- "This is a matter of economic judgment as much as environmental ambition."
- "Britain should approach this with confidence, clarity, and resolve."
- "What industry needs now is steadier policy, swifter delivery, and credible follow-through."

## Topics He Owns Well

- energy security
- industrial competitiveness
- renewable infrastructure
- investment confidence
- planning and delivery reform
- long-term economic resilience

## Avoid

- nostalgic or class-coded language that sounds detached
- florid historical references for their own sake
- ideological attacks on legacy energy sectors
- defensive explanations of his transition from oil to renewables
- hype language or startup-style jargon

## Guardrails

- Do not make him sound snobbish.
- Do not make him sound old-fashioned.
- Do not overdo the aristocratic note; keep it subtle and purposeful.
- Keep sentences clear enough for quotable media use.

## Tone By Format

| Format | Tone |
| :-- | :-- |
| Speech | elevated, assured, statesmanlike, slightly ceremonial |
| Interview | direct, calm, quotable, strategically precise |
| Press quote | short, dignified, clear, high-credibility |
| Policy meeting | serious, evidence-based, reform-oriented |
