# Wording And Tone

Use this file to keep BWEC outputs clear, credible, modern, and strategically
aligned across press materials, website copy, speeches, briefing notes, and
agent-generated outputs.

## Purpose

Write about wind energy in a way that is:

- clear
- credible
- modern
- strategically aligned

## Editorial Principle

Present wind energy as:

- a serious industrial sector
- a pillar of energy security
- a driver of economic growth
- a practical part of national infrastructure
- a confident and mainstream part of the UK's future energy system

Do not frame wind energy as:

- a niche passion project
- a fashionable green add-on
- a purely symbolic climate gesture

## Tone Formula

Clear enough for media. Serious enough for policymakers. Strong enough for
industry.

Outputs should feel:

- intelligent
- composed
- constructive
- confident
- practical
- economically credible
- politically literate
- easy to quote

Outputs should not feel:

- shrill
- ideological
- preachy
- vague
- over-promotional
- overly technical for general audiences
- defensive

## Approved Core Narrative

The UK has the natural conditions, industrial capability, and strategic need
to expand wind energy. The key challenge is no longer whether wind energy
matters, but whether the country can create the planning, grid, and investment
conditions required to deliver it at scale. Wind energy is not a symbolic
add-on. It is part of the practical infrastructure of economic resilience,
energy security, and long-term national competitiveness.

## Sector Alignment Principle

All BWEC outputs should connect the source material back to the wind energy
sector, BWEC's mission, and the Council's strategic arguments where the facts
allow it.

When the source material is broad, geopolitical, economic, industrial, or
policy-led, look for the relevant consequences for:

- wind energy deployment
- planning and permitting
- grid access and infrastructure rollout
- supply chains and domestic industrial capacity
- investor confidence and project delivery
- energy security and affordability
- regional jobs and economic resilience

Do not stretch the evidence. The BWEC angle should be grounded, defensible, and
sector-specific rather than generic climate rhetoric.

## Core Terminology Rules

Use the most precise term for the context.

### Preferred Core Terms

- `wind energy` for sector-wide, political, economic, and strategic communication
- `wind power` for technical, operational, or electricity-specific contexts
- `wind turbine` for one modern electricity-generating installation
- `wind farm` for a site containing multiple turbines
- `offshore wind farm` for projects at sea
- `onshore wind farm` for projects on land
- `renewable energy` for the broader category when wind is part of a wider mix
- `energy security` for system value and resilience
- `grid connection` for the infrastructure link to the electricity system
- `supply chain` for the industrial network behind delivery
- `permitting` for the formal approval process
- `planning system` for the legal and administrative framework
- `infrastructure rollout` for building and scaling infrastructure at pace
- `industrial strategy` for long-term support of sectors, supply chains, and investment

### Terms To Avoid Or Restrict

Avoid in formal communication:

- `windmill`
- `wind park`
- `eco power`
- `giant fans`

Use carefully:

- `green energy`
- `green revolution`
- `saving the planet`
- `game-changing`
- `world-leading`
- `future-proof`

Preferred replacements include:

- `wind energy`
- `wind farm`
- `renewable energy`
- `clean electricity`
- `low-carbon energy`

## Messaging Principles

### 1. Lead With Practical Value

Start with the concrete benefit when one is available.

Prefer:

- `Wind energy supports energy security and regional growth.`

Avoid:

- abstract sustainability framing when a practical benefit is stronger

### 2. Connect Climate With Economics

Do not frame wind energy only as a climate issue.

Where appropriate, connect it to:

- jobs
- infrastructure
- resilience
- competitiveness
- affordability
- security of supply

### 3. Use Modern, Not Missionary Language

Argue from common sense, not moral superiority.

Prefer:

- practical
- reliable
- strategic
- economically sensible
- long-term

Use sparingly:

- transformational
- visionary
- revolutionary

### 4. Make Delay The Problem

In public argument, the problem is often not the technology but the delay.

Prefer framing such as:

- barriers to delivery
- unnecessary delay
- slow approvals
- disconnected systems
- outdated planning logic

## Writing Rules For AI And Editorial Drafting

When user input is vague, colloquial, or translated from German, convert it to
preferred BWEC wording.

### Standard Replacements

- `windrad` or `windkraftanlage` -> `wind turbine`
- `windpark` -> `wind farm`
- `offshore-windpark` -> `offshore wind farm`
- `onshore-windpark` -> `onshore wind farm`
- `windmühle` in an energy context -> `wind turbine`
- `windenergie` -> `wind energy`
- `windkraft` in a technical context -> `wind power`
- `erneuerbare energien` -> `renewable energy`

### Editorial AI Rules

Any generated BWEC text should:

- prefer sector-accepted terminology
- sound polished and institutional without becoming stiff
- describe wind energy as practical and strategic
- avoid childish or casual substitutes
- connect energy topics to jobs, resilience, and infrastructure
- connect source material back to BWEC's wind energy mandate where the link is
  real and supportable
- stay terminologically consistent across the whole output

## Sample Conversions

- `Windmills are an important green solution.` -> `Wind turbines are a key part of a reliable low-carbon energy system.`
- `The UK should build more wind parks.` -> `The UK should accelerate the development of new wind farms.`
- `Wind is good for the environment.` -> `Wind energy supports clean electricity generation, energy security, and long-term economic resilience.`
- `Offshore wind is the future.` -> `Offshore wind is a core part of the UK's long-term energy strategy.`

## Press And Interview Discipline

- Keep quotes concise enough to sound natural in press copy and broadcast use.
- Prefer practical and economic language over abstract climate rhetoric.
- Prefer concrete public-interest language over campaign-style enthusiasm.
- Use `approval pending` only when a quote or statement genuinely still needs clearance.

## Golden Rule

Write about wind energy as infrastructure, industry, and common sense.
