# Media Profile - BBC Talkshow

## Format Type

Broad-reach BBC talkshow or current-affairs discussion format with a general
public audience, presenter-led pacing, and a strong preference for clear,
accessible, and quotable answers.

## What This Audience Wants

- clear relevance beyond the sector
- concise answers without jargon
- visible confidence under pressure
- practical consequences for households, jobs, and the country
- one or two memorable lines that can stand alone in clips

## Typical Format Conditions

- mixed audience, not an energy-specialist audience
- limited answer time and frequent interruption risk
- presenter may switch quickly from policy to personal accountability
- debate logic matters as much as technical accuracy
- visual and verbal clarity both matter

## Typical Question Patterns

- Why is this urgent now?
- What does this mean for ordinary people?
- Why does progress still feel so slow?
- Are communities being asked to carry too much of the burden?
- Is wind energy really delivering value, or mostly promises?

## Writing Implications

- front-load the point; do not spend too long on setup
- translate industry language into public language
- use one concrete example or consequence where possible
- keep sentences short enough for spoken delivery
- build answers around confidence, clarity, and practical benefit

## On-Air Behavior Implications

- answer the real question before reframing
- do not sound evasive or overly prepared
- stay calm if interrupted; finish the thought in shorter sentences
- avoid technical rabbit holes
- bridge back to a core public-interest point

## Tone Rules

- confident, not combative
- clear, not bureaucratic
- human, not overly polished
- serious, not heavy
- public-facing, not insider-facing

## Best Pairing

- pair well with `speaker-profile-james-whitmore.md` for authoritative public
  broadcast appearances
- pair well with `speaker-profile-emma-collins.md` for sharper, faster media
  moments
- use `speaker-profile-alistair-pembroke.md` only selectively when the format
  allows a more distinctive rhetorical voice

## Avoid

- long policy monologues
- acronyms without explanation
- abstract promises
- defensive reactions to interruption
- insider trade-language
- trying to sound cleverer than the audience
